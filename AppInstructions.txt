The core move: the app should not be a streak toy with Korean sprinkled on top. It should be a fluency operating system: curriculum spine, memory engine, listening lab, speaking lab, graded reading, and human/AI feedback all feeding one learner model.
Korean Fluency App Blueprint 🇰🇷📱
Evidence-based, mobile-first, built phase by phase

The app should be built around one brutal truth: Korean fluency is not achieved by cute daily taps alone. The app needs to behave less like a game and more like a tiny Seoul language institute living in your phone, with a calendar, a memory engine, a speech coach, and a slightly judgmental but helpful owl-free conscience.

The goal: take English speakers from zero to independent Korean use in 24 months, aiming for roughly CEFR B2 / TOPIK 4-ish functional fluency, with strong reading and listening, and practical speaking. CEFR B2 is the level where a learner can interact with enough fluency and spontaneity that regular interaction with native speakers becomes possible without strain.
1. Core product thesis
The app should not be “Duolingo for Korean”

It should be:

    A Korean fluency operating system for English speakers.

That means it must combine:
System	Purpose
Structured curriculum	Prevents random studying and “I know 900 words but cannot order soup” disease.
SRS memory engine	Keeps vocabulary, grammar, spelling, particles, and sound rules alive.
Listening lab	Trains real Korean speed, sound changes, intonation, and transcript use.
Speaking lab	Forces active output from day one.
Pronunciation engine	Fixes English-speaker Korean sound problems early.
Graded reader library	Bridges textbook Korean to real Korean.
Human/AI correction layer	Converts passive knowledge into usable Korean.
Assessment dashboard	Shows CEFR/TOPIK-style readiness, not just XP confetti.

The evidence supports this multi-system design. Yonsei’s regular Korean program uses 200 hours per semester across levels 1 to 6, and SNU describes Level 6 as requiring about 1,000 hours or equivalent proficiency to use Korean fluently in social activities.

Key English-speaker pain points

The app must explicitly address:

    Hangul is easy at the alphabet level but hard at real speed.

    Korean word order feels backwards at first.

    Particles feel tiny but cause nuclear-level confusion.

    Honorifics and speech levels are socially loaded.

    Korean sound changes make words look different from how they sound.

    Listening is much harder than reading.

    Speaking lags unless forced early.

    Grammar knowledge does not automatically become speech.

14. AI features

AI should not hallucinate Korean education like a raccoon with a whiteboard. It needs guardrails.
Safe AI uses
Feature	AI role
Grammar explanation	Explain approved curriculum points.
Sentence correction	Correct learner output.
Conversation practice	Roleplay within known level.
Feedback summary	Tag recurring errors.
Personalization	Suggest review tasks.
Tutor handoff	Generate report for human tutor.
Writing assistant	Give corrections and alternatives.
Dangerous AI uses
Risk	Control
Wrong grammar explanations	Use curriculum database and retrieval.
Too advanced Korean	Level-lock output.
Over-correction	Prioritize top 3 mistakes.
Fake native nuance	Escalate to human/native review.
Learner dependence	Require unaided production first.

19. Content licensing strategy

Do not scrape K-drama subtitles, TTMIK material, textbook audio, or app content. Copyright generally protects original written work, sound recordings, film, broadcasts, software, web content, and databases, and prevents copying, distribution, adaptation, and online use without permission.
Safer content sources
Content type	Strategy

Audio	Hire native speakers
Graded readers	Commission or license
Culture articles	Write in-house
Native clips	License or use original content
Open content	Use Creative Commons correctly

Creative Commons BY 4.0 allows sharing and adaptation, even commercially, but requires attribution and compliance with license terms.

25. The final product vision

The finished app should feel like this:

    Every day, the learner opens the app and knows exactly what to do.
    They review what they are about to forget.
    They hear Korean.
    They read Korean.
    They speak Korean.
    They get corrected.
    Their mistakes return as future practice.
    Their study plan adapts.
    Their Korean becomes less decorative and more alive.

That is the app.

Not a streak machine.
Not a phrasebook with confetti.
A proper Korean fluency engine with wheels, teeth, lungs, and a tiny polite bow. 🫰
